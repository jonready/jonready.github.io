Two Ancient 3090s vs a $9,000 Blackwell Card

June 23, 2026

A while back I wrote up the rig under my desk — two NVLink-bridged RTX 3090s, a Threadripper that was old when I bought it, and a stubborn refusal to keep paying API bills. The 3090 is a 2020 card. The dies are five years old. So when I got temporary access to a box with two RTX PRO 6000 Blackwell Server Edition cards — the current top of NVIDIA's professional stack, roughly $9,000 each — I expected the benchmark to be a humiliation.

It wasn't. The headline, which I'll spend the rest of the post defending and then bounding: for the workload I actually run, two five-year-old 3090s are about 60% as fast as one of the most expensive GPUs you can buy. And there's a real, boring reason why — it's not a fluke.

Look at the bandwidth row, because it's the whole story. Token generation — the part you feel when you're chatting with a model — is almost entirely memory-bandwidth-bound. Every token, you stream the active weights out of VRAM. And two 3090s, ganged together, push more aggregate bandwidth than a single PRO 6000. The 2020 silicon and the 2025 silicon are, on the one axis that governs decode speed, basically tied. NVIDIA spent five years and a lot of die area on things that don't help you generate the next token.

The benchmark

Same methodology as the original rig post: vllm bench serve against a 35B-A3B MoE (the same model class I run day to day), measuring single-stream latency, parallel throughput, and prefill. My rig's numbers are from that post — Int4, 16-bit KV. The Blackwell numbers I measured myself on the new box with NVFP4 weights and a matching 16-bit KV cache. I ran the 35B on a single PRO 6000, because the whole point is "my two cheap cards vs one of their expensive ones."

Across the decode metrics that's a band of roughly 56–71%. Call it 60%. A pair of cards I could buy used for about $1,600 delivers 60% of the token throughput of a $9,000 GPU. On dollars-per-token for short-prompt serving, the old rig doesn't just compete — it wins, and it isn't close.

Where the wheels come off

I'd be doing the same thing I accused the "smaller dense model" of in the last post — quoting the flattering number and hiding the rest — if I stopped there. The 60% holds for one specific shape of work: short prompts, lots of generated tokens. The moment the workload changes, so does the verdict.

Prefill is a massacre

Prefill — chewing through the prompt before the first token — is compute-bound, not bandwidth-bound, and this is where Blackwell's FP4 tensor cores show up to work. At an 8K-token prompt my rig does about 2,000 tok/s of prefill. The PRO 6000 does about 34,900.

That's not 60%. That's roughly 6% — a 17× gap. For chat, prefill is a rounding error. For RAG, long-context summarization, or any agentic loop that re-reads a big context every step, prefill is the workload, and the old rig falls off a cliff. The cheap-hardware story is really a "your prompts are short" story.

And there's a model it simply can't load

While I had the box I also ran a 122B-A10B MoE in NVFP4 — 71GB of weights, native FP4 on both Blackwell cards. It served 102 tok/s single-stream and held a 3.2M-token KV cache. My rig's verdict on this model isn't a percentage. It's a 71GB-of-weights-into-48GB-of-VRAM error. You can't run it at all. 192GB of pooled VRAM doesn't make the Blackwell box "better" here so much as it puts it in a different category — it can hold models my rig will never see.

A note on the quant, and on fp8 KV

Two honesty notes for anyone trying to reproduce this. First, the quants aren't identical and can't be: Ampere has no FP4, so my rig runs GPTQ-Int4 while the Blackwell box runs NVFP4. Same 4 bits per weight, different math. For the 35B, NVFP4's linear layers are weight-only (W4A16) and actually fall back to a Marlin kernel even on Blackwell; the 122B runs true native FP4. So "NVFP4" isn't one thing.

Second, I re-ran the Blackwell numbers with an fp8 KV cache, since that's what the model card recommends. At these batch sizes it was a wash on speed — every throughput metric landed within run-to-run noise. What fp8 KV actually bought was capacity: about 1.8× more KV cache (2.9M → 5.2M tokens on the 35B). It's a headroom upgrade for high concurrency or long context, not a speed knob. So the comparison above, on 16-bit KV both sides, is the fair one.

One more caveat against my own favor: the rented box is a cloud instance whose container blocked direct GPU-to-GPU P2P, so its two-card runs route tensor-parallel traffic through host memory and are slower than NVLink hardware would be. That only handicaps the Blackwell multi-GPU numbers, though — the single-card 35B comparison, the one the headline rests on, is unaffected.

The takeaway

I went in expecting the five-year-old cards to get embarrassed and came out thinking the opposite: Ampere's memory bandwidth aged remarkably well, and modern MoE models — with their tiny active parameter sets — happen to lean on exactly that strength. I bought bandwidth in 2020 and the workload walked over to meet it. For local chat and grinding through agentic jobs with modest prompts, two used 3090s at ~60% of a flagship's decode is the best dollar-for-dollar bet in the building.

The fair way to say it: these aren't a fast card and a slow card. They're two machines built for different jobs that happen to overlap on the cheap one. The PRO 6000 box is for the 35,000 tok/s prefill and the 122B model — neither of which my rig can touch. But if your prompts are short and your models fit in 48GB, the expensive hardware is solving a problem you don't have. The day I need a 200B model or 30k-token prompts in the loop, I'll rent Blackwell. Until then, the 3090s stay under the desk, and they've earned it.
